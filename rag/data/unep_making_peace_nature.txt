UNEP — Making Peace with Nature (2021)
Source: United Nations Environment Programme, Nairobi, 2021

OVERVIEW

The world is facing a triple planetary crisis: climate change, biodiversity loss, and pollution. These three crises are interconnected and must be addressed together. Human activities have fundamentally altered 75% of land surfaces, 66% of marine environments, and more than 85% of wetlands.

CLIMATE CHANGE AND INDIVIDUAL ACTION

The science is clear: to avoid the worst impacts of climate change, global greenhouse gas emissions must be cut by 45% by 2030 compared to 2010 levels, and reach net zero by 2050. Every fraction of a degree of warming matters.

Quantified lifestyle changes and their CO2 impact:

TRANSPORT CHOICES

Aviation is one of the most carbon-intensive activities an individual can undertake:
- A single return long-haul flight can produce more CO2e than six months of average European per-capita emissions from all other activities combined
- Business class air travel has approximately 3× the climate impact of economy class
- Choosing train over plane for journeys under 1,500 km can save 80–95% of emissions
- Example: Paris to London — train emits 6 kg CO2e vs. aircraft 54 kg CO2e (a 90% saving)
- Example: Delhi to Mumbai — train (Rajdhani) emits ~55 kg CO2e vs. flight ~338 kg CO2e

Electric vehicles charged on renewable electricity have a lifecycle carbon footprint 70–90% lower than petrol vehicles. Even on India's current coal-heavy grid, an EV emits approximately 70% less CO2 per km than a petrol car.

DIETARY CHOICES

Food systems contribute 21–37% of global greenhouse gas emissions. Diet changes are one of the most powerful levers individuals have:

- Global average food-related emissions: approximately 2–3 tCO2e per person per year
- Moving to a predominantly plant-based diet could reduce personal food-related emissions by up to 73% in high-income countries
- Reducing meat consumption to once per week saves approximately 0.5 tCO2e per year for an average person
- Food waste represents approximately 8–10% of global greenhouse gas emissions; halving food waste at household level saves approximately 0.1–0.3 tCO2e per year

Meat consumption comparison (per kg of protein):
- Beef: 60–100 kgCO2e (highest)
- Lamb: 25 kgCO2e
- Pork: 7 kgCO2e
- Chicken: 6 kgCO2e
- Fish (farmed): 5 kgCO2e
- Eggs: 4.5 kgCO2e
- Dairy: 3.2 kgCO2e
- Legumes (lentils, beans): 0.9 kgCO2e (lowest)

ENERGY AT HOME

Residential buildings account for approximately 17–19% of global CO2 emissions. Key actions:
- Switching from a gas/LPG water heater to solar: saves ~200–400 kgCO2e per year in India
- Installing LED lighting throughout a home: saves ~150 kgCO2e per year
- Reducing air conditioning by 1°C in summer: saves approximately 5% of AC electricity use
- Washing clothes in cold water vs. hot: saves approximately 40 kgCO2e per year

CONSCIOUS CONSUMPTION

Manufacturing and consumption of goods account for approximately 11% of global CO2 emissions. High-impact purchasing decisions:
- One smartphone purchase: ~70 kgCO2e (mostly manufacturing)
- One return flight avoided: 500–2,000 kgCO2e
- Buying second-hand clothing instead of new: saves ~65% of clothing emissions
- Repairing electronics rather than replacing: can halve lifecycle emissions

SYSTEMS THINKING

Individual action is necessary but not sufficient. The UNEP report emphasizes that:
1. Individual choices must be supported by systemic change (policies, infrastructure, markets)
2. High-income individuals have disproportionate carbon footprints — the top 1% globally emit more than 2× the bottom 50% combined
3. India's development path choices will significantly influence global emissions — but low per-capita emissions must not be confused with low absolute emissions as the economy grows

THE ROLE OF NATURE

Protecting and restoring nature is critical for carbon sequestration:
- Tropical forests store approximately 250 billion tonnes of carbon (live trees alone)
- One hectare of intact tropical forest sequesters approximately 5–8 tCO2/year
- Mangrove forests sequester carbon at 3–5× the rate of tropical forests per hectare
- Restoring degraded forests could provide approximately 1/3 of the climate mitigation needed by 2030

Avoided deforestation and reforestation could provide 30% of the cost-effective mitigation required to limit warming to 2°C.
